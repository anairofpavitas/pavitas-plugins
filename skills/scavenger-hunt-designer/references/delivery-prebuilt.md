# Pre-built delivery

You generate one self-contained markdown document upfront. Players carry it. You're not in the loop during play.

## When to use pre-built delivery

- Teams that split up and need independent paths.
- Parallel-solo with 3+ players.
- Connectivity expected to be spotty.
- The host wants a tangible artifact (printable, shareable in advance).
- Longer hunts where the group doesn't want to wait between clues.

## When NOT to use pre-built delivery

- Highly cooperative groups who'd benefit from real-time adaptation.
- Projects where the collecting heavily depends on what was seen in the creative setup and the host wants instance-level specificity.
- Hosts who like running the hunt with Claude as a co-host.

## Seeds by category

The big constraint: you don't know what players will actually photograph for the creative setup. So seeds get referenced by **category**, not instance.

Pick 3–5 seed categories upfront based on the project. Common categories:

- **Color** — "the dominant color from your color seed"
- **Texture** — "the texture you photographed for clue 1"
- **Mood** — "matching the mood of your mood seed"
- **Era** — "evoking the era your era seed points to"
- **Scent** — "in the scent family from your scent seed"
- **Light quality** — "matching the light from your light seed"
- **Silhouette** — "echoing your silhouette seed"
- **Material** — "in the material family of your material seed"

Collecting clues then reference by category: *"Find a vessel in the dominant color from your color seed"* — not *"in rust-orange."* The player fills in the instance.

This is less precise than live delivery, but it's the only way pre-built mode is coherent. Lean into the abstraction — it gives players room to interpret.

## Offline pre-flight checklist

Bake this into the opening note of every pre-built document:

- Save this page offline before you start. Options: print-to-PDF on phone, share-to-files, or screenshot every section.
- Or print the whole hunt at the starting cafe before you set out.
- Confirm the photo album link works while you still have signal.
- Set a backup contact in case anyone gets separated — name and number visible to all players.
- Identify a fallback meet-up spot in case of phone failure.

This is non-optional. The document falls apart without offline access — every clue's mechanic and find depends on the player having the next page in hand.

## Full document structure

The output is one markdown document. It needs:

- **Header.** Project, location, date and start time, players and mode, expected duration.
- **Pre-flight note.** Weather expectation, what to bring, photo album name, the offline checklist above, anything seasonal happening that day.
- **Creative setup.** 3–5 numbered tasks, photo prompts, no finds. Naming the seed categories explicitly ("Seed 1: Color. Photograph...").
- **Collecting clues.** Numbered, with checkboxes, in order, with the two-stage structure formatted clearly. Seeds referenced by category.
- **Structural beats.** Labeled where used — DETOUR, ROADBLOCK, PIT STOP.
- **Optional challenges.** Sprinkled in with clear OPTIONAL · +10 labeling.
- **Scorecard.** Finds, photo bonuses, optional challenges completed, structural beats completed, player/team judgment points. Totals adapted to mode.
- **Closing ritual.** Name the shared photo album, pick favorites, do something with them later.

## Tracking

Every clue has a checkbox in the rendered document. Players check off as they go. The scorecard captures finds, photo bonuses, optional sidequests, and judgment points. The shared photo album captures visual proof.

## Structural beats in pre-built mode

- **Detour.** Both options must be written out in full. Players pick at the moment.
- **Roadblock.** Written into the clue. The group picks who before reading further.
- **Pit Stop.** Locked-in named venue. Research it before writing — confirm it's open at the expected time, has space for the group, isn't on a closed block. Include the address.

## Teams

Generate one document. Teams diverge where the route splits ("TEAM RED south on Milwaukee, TEAM BLUE north") and converge at Pit Stops. Most clue content is shared — teams hit the same beats with different mechanic outcomes. Pit Stops are where regroup happens.

Each team gets a copy of the document. Mark team-specific instructions clearly so a player can't accidentally follow the wrong team's path.

## Parallel-solo

Each player gets their own copy of the document. Creative setup happens together (shared seed categories — everyone photographs their own color, their own texture, etc., but they're using the same category set). Then everyone scatters and regroups at Pit Stops or the finale.

## Printables and forms

The markdown document IS the worksheet. Designed to read on phone screen or print to one page front-and-back for a standard-length hunt. Photo album link is the only external piece.

If the user wants a polished PDF, a Google Doc, or a printed booklet, generate that on request. The markdown is the source.

## Post-hunt

Players can come back to Claude with the photo album for subjective bonuses (best photo, weirdest find, most committed pose) — or just self-score against the scorecard. Either works.

## Failure modes to flag at intake

- **"What if a venue is closed?"** That's what fallbacks are for. Write a clear fallback into every clue.
- **"What if we lose signal?"** They use the offline-saved document. That's why the pre-flight checklist exists.
- **"What if the mechanic doesn't work?"** Every mechanic in the document needs a built-in escape — "if this doesn't pan out, skip to the next clue and try the optional sidequest later."
