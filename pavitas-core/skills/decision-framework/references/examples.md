# Decision Framework Examples

This file shows real-world examples of how each framework is applied to actual decisions.

## Example 1: Year-to-Live Clarifier

### Scenario: Should I accept a promotion?

```markdown
# Year-to-Live Decision: Senior Manager Promotion

## The Setup
Today's date: October 18, 2025
My last day: October 18, 2026
Time remaining: 365 days

## The Brutal Question
If I only had 365 days left to live, would I spend ANY time preparing for this promotion and taking on these responsibilities?

**Honest answer**: NO

## The Priority Stack
Knowing my time is limited, here's what ACTUALLY matters:

1. **Non-negotiables** (Things I'd do even with 1 week left)
   - Time with my partner (14 hours/week - just being present)
   - Creative writing that has nothing to do with career (10 hours/week)
   - Meaningful conversations with friends (8 hours/week)

2. **Important but flexible** (Things I'd do with 6+ months)
   - Teaching/mentoring others (5 hours/week)
   - Building something that outlasts me (10 hours/week)
   - Travel to places I've always wanted to see (varies)

3. **Nice to have** (Things I'd skip entirely)
   - Status/title advancement
   - Impressive resume bullets
   - Fixing my parents' disappointment about my choices
   - Keeping up with tech trends I don't actually care about

## This Decision Specifically
**Does this decision serve my non-negotiables?**: NO
**If yes, how?**: N/A
**If no, why am I considering it?**: Status, money, fear of seeming "unambitious"

## The Counterfactual
On my last day, will I regret:
- **Saying YES to this**: Yes. I'll regret spending my final year in more meetings, managing people instead of doing creative work, and optimizing for a future I won't have.
- **Saying NO to this**: No. I can't imagine lying on my deathbed wishing I'd taken that promotion.

## Decision
**I will**: NO
**Because**: This serves my ego and future-planning, not my actual life.

## 6-Month Check-in Trigger
April 18, 2026 - Review: Did I honor this decision? Am I still aligned with my non-negotiables?
```

**Outcome**: Turned down promotion. Six months later: No regrets. Used the extra time for creative projects and relationships.

## Example 2: Narrative Reality Check

### Scenario: "I need to work 60+ hours/week to be successful"

```markdown
# Narrative Reality Check: Work Hours for Success

## My Story
**What I tell myself**: "I need to work at least 60 hours per week to be successful. All the people I admire work insane hours. If I'm only working 40 hours, I'm being lazy and won't achieve my goals. I have to grind now to succeed later."

## The Evidence Audit

**Calendar review**: [Pulled last 90 days]
- Average work hours per week: 62 hours
- Highest productivity weeks (measured by output): 45-50 hours
- Lowest productivity weeks: 65+ hours
- Pattern: More hours = more time in meetings, less deep work

**Email/communication patterns**:
- Most thoughtful work happens: 9am-1pm (4 hours/day)
- After 6pm: Emails get progressively worse, more mistakes
- Weekend work: Mostly redoing things from Friday afternoon fatigue

**Past behavior**:
- Last time I maintained 60+ hours: 8 months in 2024
- What happened: Burnout in month 9, took 3 weeks to recover
- Actual output over 12 months: Same as previous year when I worked 45 hours/week
- Major project success: Happened during a 42-hour work week with deep focus

## The Gap Analysis
**Story**: "I need 60+ hours to be successful"
**Reality**: "My best work happens in focused 40-50 hour weeks. Extra hours mostly generate busywork and errors."
**Gap**: I'm conflating hours with productivity because I feel guilty when not working.

## The Adjusted Narrative
Based on evidence, here's what's ACTUALLY true:

"I produce my best work in 45-50 focused hours per week. Beyond that, I'm in diminishing returns territory—making mistakes, attending low-value meetings, and generating work that I later have to redo. My story about needing 60+ hours comes from comparing myself to others' public performance, not from my actual productivity data. The people I admire probably have support systems and advantages I'm not seeing."

## Decision Implications
**Original decision based on story**: Continue working 60+ hours, feel guilty about taking breaks
**Revised decision based on reality**: Cap at 50 hours, focus on deep work quality, track output not hours
**Why this matters**: Prevents burnout, improves actual output, stops the shame spiral

## Reality-Test Commitment
**Next time I tell myself this story, I will**:
- Check my calendar for the past 30 days
- Identify my 3 best pieces of work and note the hours worked that week
- Ask: "Is this shame talking or is this data?"

## 90-Day Check-in Trigger
January 18, 2026 - Review: Am I still believing the story or living the reality?
```

**Outcome**: Reduced to 48 hours/week. Output quality improved. No negative career impact. The shame was the problem, not the hours.

## Example 3: 7-Day Cooling-Off Protocol

### Scenario: $1,200 espresso machine (already own $400 machine)

```markdown
# 7-Day Cooling-Off: Breville Oracle Touch

**Date started**: October 18, 2025
**Decision deadline**: October 25, 2025
**Price**: $1,899 (on sale from $2,499)
**Discount**: $600 / 24% (Expires: October 22, 2025)

## The Discount Interrogation

### Would you buy this at full price?
**Answer**: NO
**Why this matters**: I'm buying the discount, not the machine. If I wouldn't pay $2,499, then I don't want it enough at any price.

### How often does this brand run sales?
**Research findings**: Checked last 12 months - sale every 8 weeks
**Pattern identified**: This "limited time" sale happens 6x per year
**Implication**: Not urgent. Will be on sale again by December.

### What's the actual $ saved vs opportunity cost?
**Discount amount**: $600
**Hours spent researching**: 8 hours (YouTube reviews, Reddit, comparison shopping)
**Effective hourly rate of "savings"**: $600 / 8 hours = $75/hour
**Your actual hourly value**: $125/hour
**Reality check**: I'm paying myself less than my rate to "save" money.

## The Pattern Analysis
**Similar past purchases**: 
1. Air fryer #2 - $300 (was $450): Used 5 times, then back to air fryer #1
2. Standing desk converter - $280 (was $400): Used 2 weeks, caused back pain
3. Noise-canceling headphones #3 - $350 (was $450): Same as headphones #2, now in drawer
4. Air fryer #3 & #4 - Don't want to talk about it

For each:
- Still use it?: Mostly no
- Did features matter?: No, aspirational use only
- Trigger?: Discount + YouTuber enthusiasm

**Pattern identified**: I buy discounted "upgrades" hoping they'll change my behavior. They never do.

## The Incremental Value Test
**What I already own**: Breville Barista Express ($400)
**What it can do**: Makes excellent espresso, steam milk, adjustable grind
**What the Oracle adds**: Automatic milk texturing, digital temp control, easier cleanup

**Honest assessment**: 
- Features I'll actually use: Easier cleanup (maybe)
- Features I'll never use: All the digital presets (I'd still manually dial it in)
- Net new capability: Saves ~2 minutes per drink cleanup

**$/Quality-of-Life ratio**: 
- Net cost: $1,899 - $200 (resale value) = $1,699
- Actual new capability: 2 minutes saved per drink
- Expected uses: 2 drinks/day = 60/month
- Time saved: 120 minutes/month = 2 hours/month
- Cost per hour saved: $1,699 / (2 hours/month * 24 months) = $35/hour saved

**Reality check**: I'm paying $35/hour to save time I currently spend doing something I enjoy (making espresso is my morning ritual).

## The Regret Projection

### If I DON'T buy this:
**In 7 days**: Mild FOMO, but will forget about it
**In 6 months**: Won't remember the model name
**In 2 years**: Will never think about it

### If I DO buy this:
**In 7 days**: Excited, will use it daily
**In 6 months**: Back to same routine, automated features feel soulless
**In 2 years**: Regret spending $1,699 for identical espresso

## Fast-Track Override Option
- [ ] I've answered all questions above honestly ✓
- [ ] I would buy this at full price ✗
- [ ] This provides genuine new capability, not incremental improvement ✗
- [ ] Missing this opportunity creates real cost, not just FOMO ✗
- [ ] I've documented what I'm sacrificing by rushing ✗

**1 out of 5 checked. Wait the full 7 days.**

## Day 7 Decision
**I will**: NOT BUY
**Because**: I'm in love with the idea of effortless espresso, but the manual process is actually part of what I enjoy. This is air fryer #5 waiting to happen.

## 30-Day Review Trigger
November 18, 2025 - Review: Do I miss it? Did another "limited time" sale happen? What did I learn?
```

**Outcome**: Didn't buy it. Sale came back 6 weeks later. Still didn't want it. Saved $1,699 and learned the pattern.

## Example 4: Time Allocation Audit

### Scenario: Should I start a podcast?

```markdown
# Time Allocation Audit: Starting a Podcast

## The Fantasy Budget
**What I THINK my time looks like**:

| Activity | Hours/Week |
|----------|------------|
| Deep work | 25 |
| Meetings | 15 |
| Email/admin | 10 |
| Exercise | 7 |
| Family time | 20 |
| Hobby projects | 8 |
| Sleep | 56 |
| Other | 27 |
| Total: | 168 hours |

## The Reality Audit
**Pulled actual data from**: Calendar (last 30 days) + Screen time + Email timestamps

**What my time ACTUALLY looks like**:

| Activity | Hours/Week | Gap from Fantasy |
|----------|------------|------------------|
| Deep work | 12 | -13 hours |
| Meetings | 22 | +7 hours |
| Email/admin | 18 | +8 hours |
| Exercise | 3 | -4 hours |
| Family time | 14 | -6 hours |
| Hobby projects | 2 | -6 hours |
| Screen time (social media) | 12 | +12 hours (didn't budget) |
| Sleep | 52 | -4 hours |
| Other | 33 | +6 hours |
| Total: | 168 hours | |

## The Insight Layer
**Biggest surprises**:
1. I'm spending 12 hours/week on social media when I thought I spent "basically none"
2. Deep work is HALF of what I think it is
3. I'm in 7 more hours of meetings than I thought

**Time sinks** (things taking more time than they're worth):
- Social media: 12 hours → Getting nothing of value, pure dopamine trap
- Meetings that could be emails: ~8 hours → Destroying deep work time

**Time gems** (things providing outsized value):
- Deep work mornings (the 12 hours I do get): Produce 80% of my actual output
- Family dinners: Only time I'm truly present

## This Decision's True Cost
**What podcast requires**: 8-10 hours/week
- Recording: 2 hours
- Editing: 3 hours
- Promotion: 2 hours
- Research/prep: 2-3 hours

**Where will those hours come from?**
| Activity to reduce | Current hours | New hours | What I lose |
|-------------------|---------------|-----------|-------------|
| Social media | 12 | 4 | The doom scroll (good riddance) |
| Hobby projects | 2 | 0 | The thing I said I value but don't do |
| Deep work | 12 | 10 | 2 hours of my most productive time |

**Be specific**: I'll block social media apps and trade 2 hours of morning deep work for podcast work.

## The Sustainability Test
**Can I sustain this for 6 months?**
- [✗] I've identified specific hours to trade - Yes, but...
- [✗] The trade-off is worth it based on my priorities - Trading deep work is not aligned with priorities
- [✗] I've accounted for energy/context-switching costs - Podcast prep will fragment my mornings
- [✗] I have a plan for what I'll stop doing - Yes, social media is gone

**3 out of 4 boxes unchecked. This won't work.**

## Decision
**I will**: NO (or DEFER until I actually reclaim the social media time first)
**Time trade**: First prove I can kill social media for 60 days, THEN reconsider podcast
**Review trigger**: December 18, 2025

## 30-Day Reality Check
November 18, 2025
- Did I actually stop social media? [CHECK DATA]
- What did I do with that time instead?
- Do I still want to do the podcast?
```

**Outcome**: Deferred. Deleted social media apps. After 60 days, realized I didn't actually want a podcast—I wanted the identity of "person who has a podcast." Never started it. No regrets.

## Example 5: Incremental Value Calculator

### Scenario: Upgrade from iPhone 14 Pro to iPhone 16 Pro

```markdown
# Incremental Value: iPhone 16 Pro

## What I Currently Have
**Item**: iPhone 14 Pro
**Paid**: $999
**Purchase date**: October 2023
**Current utility** (1-10): 9
**Current satisfaction** (1-10): 8

## What I'm Considering
**Item**: iPhone 16 Pro
**Price**: $1,099
**Key difference**: Better camera, faster chip, slightly better battery

## Feature Delta Analysis

| Feature | Current Item (14 Pro) | New Item (16 Pro) | Do I Actually Use This? |
|---------|-------------|----------|------------------------|
| Camera quality | Excellent | 15% better | NO - I use auto mode, can't tell difference in my photos |
| Processing speed | Fast | 30% faster | NO - Never notice lag on current phone |
| Battery life | Full day | 2 hours more | MAYBE - But I charge nightly anyway |
| USB-C | Lightning | USB-C | YES - But $10 cable solves this |
| AI features | None | New AI features | MAYBE - But I don't know if I'll use them |

## The Brutal Math
**Price of new item**: $1,099
**Resale value of current item**: $450
**Net cost of upgrade**: $649

**Actual new capabilities** (features I'll use that current item can't do): 1 (USB-C convenience)
**Cost per new capability**: $649 / 1 = $649 per feature

**Expected uses per month**: Using phone daily, but "using USB-C" is not a separate activity
**Cost per use over expected lifetime**: This math doesn't make sense for an incremental upgrade

## The Downgrade Test
**If I lost my current item, would I**:
- [✓] Buy the exact same one again (iPhone 14 Pro)
- [ ] Buy the cheaper version
- [ ] Buy this "upgrade"
- [ ] Not replace it at all

**Why this matters**: I'd buy my current phone again. That means my current phone is already perfect for me.

## The Satisfaction Paradox
**Current satisfaction**: 8/10
**Expected satisfaction after upgrade**: 9/10
**Expected satisfaction gain**: +1

**Reality check from past upgrades**:
- Last upgrade: iPhone 12 → iPhone 14 Pro
- Expected satisfaction gain: +2
- Actual satisfaction gain after 6 months: 0 (returned to baseline immediately)
- Pattern: New phone excitement lasts exactly 1 week

**Research finding**: Most upgrades provide < 1 month of satisfaction boost before returning to baseline.

## Opportunity Cost
**$649 could alternatively buy**:
1. Weekend trip somewhere I've never been ($600)
2. 1 year of upgraded cloud storage I actually need ($100) + Nice headphones ($200) + Donation to charity ($349)
3. 6 months of therapy I keep saying I should do ($650)

**Which would provide more lasting value?**: All three alternatives would provide more lasting value than a slightly better phone.

## Decision
**I will**: NOT BUY
**Because**: My current phone is perfect. I'm chasing the upgrade dopamine hit, not actual utility. I would literally rather have any of the three alternatives.

**If buying**: N/A - But if I change my mind, max price is $0 because I don't actually want this.

## 6-Month Review
April 18, 2026
- Actual satisfaction vs expected: Still happy with 14 Pro (8/10)
- Features used vs purchased for: N/A (didn't buy)
- Was this worth it?: YES - Saved $649 and used it for the weekend trip. Have photos from that trip taken on my "inferior" camera that I treasure.
- What did I learn?: Every phone generation feels like a must-have upgrade until I remember my current phone already does everything I need.
```

**Outcome**: Kept iPhone 14 Pro. Two years later, still works perfectly. Saved $649 for actually meaningful experiences.

## Example 6: Pre-Commitment Post-Mortem

### Scenario: Should I join a startup as advisor?

```markdown
# Pre-Commitment Post-Mortem: Startup Advisor Role

## The Commitment
**What**: Advise early-stage fintech startup, attend monthly meetings, make intros, provide strategic guidance
**Time required**: 5 hours/month (they say), realistically 8-10 hours/month
**Duration**: October 2025 → ongoing (with 6-month review)
**Why I'm considering**: Interesting product, equity, feels good to help, FOMO on their potential success

## The Future Scenario
**It's now April 2026**. I've stopped advising this startup.

**Why did I quit?** (Be brutally honest about likely reasons)
1. Monthly meetings became "Can you hop on a quick call?" 3x per week
2. They wanted intros I couldn't make, then I felt guilty
3. Strategic advice was ignored, felt like I was shouting into void
4. The equity vesting schedule meant I got nothing for 1 year anyway
5. It was eating into my deep work time every single month

## Pattern Recognition
**Similar past commitments**:

| Commitment | Duration Planned | Duration Actual | Why I Stopped |
|------------|-----------------|-----------------|---------------|
| Board advisor for nonprofit | 2 years | 4 months | Meetings dragged on, my advice wasn't relevant, felt trapped |
| Mentor for accelerator | 6 months | 3 months | Mentees wanted me to do work for them, not advise |
| Advisory role for SaaS company | Ongoing | 7 months | CEO only wanted validation, not real advice |

**Pattern identified**: I join advisory roles out of FOMO and ego. I quit when I realize my advice isn't valued and my time is being wasted. Every single time.

## The Failure Pre-Planning
**Given my pattern, this commitment will likely fail because**:
- They'll want more time than initially scoped (happens every time)
- My advice will be ignored or they'll only want validation
- I'll feel resentful about the time investment vs lack of equity vesting
- I'll realize too late that I don't actually care about fintech

**How I'll prevent each failure mode**:
- Time creep → Hard boundary: Only monthly 1-hour calls, no "quick calls", written async advice otherwise
- Advice ignored → Establish upfront: "I give advice, you decide. If you're not implementing, let's not waste each other's time."
- Equity resentment → Negotiate: 25% vesting after 6 months, or no deal
- Don't care about fintech → **I CAN'T PREVENT THIS. I don't actually care about fintech.**

**If I can't articulate concrete prevention strategies, I should not commit.**

## The Obligation Audit
**What I'm obligating myself to**:
- Monthly strategic advice (1 hour)
- Intro to 3-5 potential customers/investors
- Review of major decisions
- Available for "emergencies"

**What happens if I quit halfway**:
- Who gets hurt: Founders (they're counting on me), their team (instability signal)
- What gets damaged: My reputation (flaky advisor), relationship with mutual connection
- How hard to extract myself: Hard - No clear exit clause, equity complications, guilt

**Exit strategy**: Set 6-month hard review date. Clear criteria for success (they implement advice, time stays within bounds, I'm energized). If not met, I exit cleanly.

## The Enthusiasm Decay Curve
**Current enthusiasm** (1-10): 8
**Expected enthusiasm in 1 month**: 6 (once initial excitement wears off)
**Expected enthusiasm in 3 months**: 4 (once I realize time commitment is higher)
**Expected enthusiasm in 6 months**: 2 (once I realize I don't care about fintech)

**At what enthusiasm level will I quit?**: 3
**When will I likely hit that threshold?**: Month 5

**Reality check**: Am I willing to continue this commitment even when enthusiasm hits 3? NO.

## The Replacement Test
**What I'll stop doing to make room for this**:
- Deep work time: From 12 hours to 10 hours per week

**Can I live with that trade for 6 months?**: NO
**What will I miss most about the thing I'm reducing?**: My deep work time is when I do my best thinking. I'm already not getting enough of it.

## Decision
**I will commit**: NO

**If YES**: N/A - All checkboxes failed

**Actual reason I'm saying no**:
- [ ] I've identified my likely failure modes ✓
- [ ] I have concrete prevention strategies ✗ (Can't prevent not caring about fintech)
- [ ] I have a clear exit strategy ✓
- [ ] I've made realistic time trades ✗ (I'm not willing to lose more deep work time)
- [ ] I'm willing to continue even when enthusiasm drops ✗ (Enthusiasm will hit quit-threshold by month 5)

**0 out of 5 boxes checked for moving forward. This is a clear NO.**

## Check-in Schedule
N/A - Not committing

**Alternative**: If I want to help, offer one-time 2-hour strategy session instead. No ongoing commitment.
```

**Outcome**: Declined advisor role. Offered one-time session instead. Felt relief immediately. Founders found someone who actually cared about fintech. My deep work time stayed intact.

## Key Insights from Examples

1. **Year-to-Live Clarifier**: Best for cutting through status/ego-driven decisions
2. **Narrative Reality Check**: Best for breaking self-deception patterns
3. **7-Day Cooling-Off**: Best for catching impulse purchasing patterns
4. **Time Allocation Audit**: Best for exposing the gap between fantasy and reality schedules
5. **Incremental Value Calculator**: Best for upgrade decisions where you already own a working version
6. **Pre-Commitment Post-Mortem**: Best for predicting why you'll quit something before you start

**Meta-pattern**: In every example, the frameworks force confrontation with actual data, past patterns, and honest feelings rather than aspirational stories.
