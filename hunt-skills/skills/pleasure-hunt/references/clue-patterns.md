# Clue patterns

A starting palette of clue shapes, plus worked examples of full hunts. Riff on these. Invent freely. The point is always concrete missions — never abstract suggestions.

---

## Clue shapes

**The timed dare.** A window and a mission. *"You have 10 minutes to find a place to [act]. Go."* The clock is the heat.

**The opportunity trigger.** Fires on a condition, not a clock. *"Next time you're both out of sight of other people, [act]."*

**The signal start.** For partners — a clue that begins with a discreet signal. *"Send him [emoji]. When it comes back, you have five minutes to get somewhere private."*

**The worn-underneath.** Fires before the hunt and runs through it. *"Put the cock ring on before you leave. It comes off at the last clue."*

**The dice roll.** Hand the choice to chance. *"Roll the sexy dice. Do what comes up. Three rolls."*

**The escalating repeat.** The same clue, raised. *"Like clue 2 — but this time, more."*

**The location hunt.** The mission is finding the place. *"Find somewhere you could have sex within 10 minutes. Not near home? Get creative — a car, a single-stall restroom, a stairwell."*

**The app spark.** Remote contact through a dating or messaging app — see `hunt-modes.md`.

**The conversation clue.** Especially for the open-conversations-with-Colin goal. *"Before the next clue, tell him one thing you've been wanting to try. Just one. See where it goes."*

**The escape hatch.** Any clue depending on another person or a public setting carries its own out. *"...if it's not landing, kiss instead and move on."*

**Named-activity spine.** When the user names an activity at intake, build the hunt's escalation *toward* it — earlier clues warm up to the named act, the peak delivers it. The named activity is the destination; the other clues are the route.

## Pacing and timers

For a hunt longer than one sitting, tell the user to set timers or alarms — each one fires the next clue. *"Set an alarm every 45 minutes. Each alarm is your next clue."* It keeps the escalation moving without clock-watching.

For a quickie, the timer lives inside the clue: *"10 minutes. Go."*

For an all-day hunt, space the clues across hours and let them breathe — six to ten clues, not twenty.

## What a hunt should not look like

- More than ten clues for anything short of a full day.
- Clues that need props or places the user can't plausibly reach.
- Abstract instructions ("be sensual," "explore intimacy") — every clue is a concrete act.
- A clue depending on another person with no fallback.
- Escalation that skips tiers or blows past the named ceiling.

When a draft hunt drifts this way, cut it back. Simple beats clever.
