# Environment context

Environment shapes everything: which clue mechanics work, which rungs are realistic, which project shapes are achievable, which seed prompts land. Match the hunt to where the user actually is.

Ask the environment at intake unless it's inferable from a named neighborhood or scale.

---

## The four environment types

### Shop-rich

Dense commercial activity. Independent shops, vintage stores, cafes, restaurants, galleries, boutique markets. High walking-shopping density.

- **Chicago examples:** Wicker Park, Andersonville, Lincoln Square, Logan Square, West Loop, Old Town, parts of West Town.
- **Best rungs:** all rungs work. Rung 4 (combinatorial) shines here.
- **Clue mechanics:** standard. Coin-flip, blind map-pin, first-letter race, shopkeeper recommendations.
- **Project shapes:** all available. Combinatorial gift bags, craft material gathering, outfit assembly, vignette/shelf moments.
- **Pacing:** clues can be short — venues are close together. 8–15 min per clue is normal.
- **Cautions:** small shops have limited space — see scavenger-hunt-designer's "cramped neighborhood" notes for solo-with-companion + dog pacing.

### Shop-sparse / residential

Houses, gardens, parks, low commercial density. A few shops scattered but not a shopping district. Walking-pleasant but not browsing-rich.

- **Chicago examples:** much of Portage Park, parts of Albany Park, Norwood Park, Sauganash, Beverly, parts of North Center, residential stretches of most neighborhoods.
- **Best rungs:** 1, 2, found-object 3, found-object 4. Standard acquisition (3/4 commercial) is limited.
- **Clue mechanics:** noticing-heavy. Path-aware clues are essential. Architecture, gardens, trees, doors, light quality, soundscape.
- **Project shapes:** found-object craft (botanical, natural assemblage), photo collage, noticing prompts. Limited gift bag unless found-object.
- **Pacing:** clues are longer — more walking between landmarks. 12–25 min per clue.
- **Seed bias:** prompts that work without commercial signage — echo, time-shift, sensory pairing, mood translation, the thing nobody looked at, negative space.

### Foraging-friendly (parks, beaches, forest preserves, gardens)

Heavy nature density. Active foraging is the natural acquisition mode. Limited commercial activity.

- **Chicago examples:** any park (Portage Park itself, Humboldt Park, Lincoln Park, Jackson Park), forest preserves (Cook County system), Lakefront Trail, North Park Village Nature Center, Garfield Park Conservatory grounds.
- **Best rungs:** 1, 2, found-object 3 and 4.
- **Clue mechanics:** path through nature, look for specific botanical or natural features. *"Find three different leaf shapes on the next 200 feet of trail. Photograph each."*
- **Project shapes:** botanical arrangement, pressed-flower-and-leaf craft, stone/wood assemblage, nature-inspired colorways for crochet/art, photo collage.
- **Pacing:** slow. Match the pace of being-outside.
- **Cautions:** respect foraging ethics — don't take from protected areas, don't take live cuttings from plants you can't identify, leave more than you take. Don't direct foraging from manicured public gardens (vs. wild edges and trails).

### Mixed / variable

The user is traveling through multiple environment types (residential → commercial → park) or in a neighborhood with patchy density.

- Common case for travel hunts and wider-range hunts.
- **Clue mechanics:** adapt per location within the hunt. The first clue might be a residential noticing prompt; the third might be a shop find; the fifth might be a park forage.
- **Project shapes:** combinatorial works if the user passes through commercial density. Otherwise lean noticing + found-object.
- **Side quest opportunities:** mixed environments are the natural home for the "person you know in the area" side quest — if the route passes near someone's home.

## Found-object foraging guide

When the rung is 3 or 4 and the environment is shop-sparse or foraging-friendly, acquisition becomes foraging. Some specifics:

**What's gettable:**
- Fallen leaves, twigs, bark scraps
- Pinecones, acorns, seedpods
- Pebbles and small stones (from public ground, not landscaped beds)
- Feathers (check local laws — some are protected in the US, e.g., migratory bird feathers)
- Fallen flowers and petals
- Sticks of various textures and sizes
- Moss and lichen scraps (sparing — slow to regenerate)
- Sea glass, shells (if near water)
- Wildflowers in unprotected areas (avoid native species in protected zones)

**What's not gettable:**
- Live cuttings from any plant in any garden, public or private
- Anything from protected areas (national forests, nature preserves with no-collect rules)
- Anything from manicured public gardens or planted parkways
- Live insects, mushrooms (unless user is an experienced forager), animal remains beyond feathers/shells

**Ethical foraging defaults:**
- Take only what's already fallen
- Leave more than you take
- Don't take from one spot — distribute the gathering
- If you're unsure, leave it

For the user specifically: check whether the area has signage about foraging. Chicago Park District has rules — public parks are generally no-collection. Forest preserves vary. Stick to publicly-walkable margins, not the heart of protected areas.

## Found-object project shapes

When the rung is 4 + foraged inputs, the combinatorial shapes shift:

### Botanical arrangement
- **Hero element:** the most striking single piece (a colorful leaf, an unusual seedpod, a curled bark scrap)
- **Supporting cast:** 2–3 contrasting textures
- **Filler:** small repeats (acorns, pinecones, small leaves) for density
- **Vessel:** something to hold it (you may need to bring this from home or improvise)

### Pressed-flower-and-leaf craft
- **Substrate:** the press itself (a book + paper, or proper press at home)
- **Specimens:** 5–8 different small specimens, photographed first
- **End product:** pressed botanicals for future cards, framed pieces, or journal embellishments

### Stone/wood assemblage
- **Anchor stones:** 2–3 stones of varied size/color
- **Connecting elements:** sticks, twigs, dried tendrils
- **Visual hook:** one unexpected element (a feather, a piece of sea glass, a leaf in the off-season color)

### Color-and-texture inspiration set
(For crochet, sketching, painting projects)

- **Color anchors:** 3–5 specimens chosen for color, photographed
- **Texture anchors:** 3–5 specimens chosen for texture
- **Composition:** the combination becomes the color/texture palette for the next project at home

## Environment + rung quick-pairing table

| Environment | Rung 1 | Rung 2 | Rung 3 | Rung 4 |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Shop-rich | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ (commercial) | ✓ (best fit) |
| Shop-sparse / residential | ✓ (default) | ✓ (default) | Found-object only | Found-object only |
| Foraging-friendly | ✓ | ✓ | Found-object (best fit) | Found-object (best fit) |
| Mixed / travel | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ (varies by section) | ✓ (best for travel hunts) |

When user-stated rung doesn't fit environment, flag it and offer alternatives. Example: user says "rung 4, gift bag, residential" — flag that commercial gift options are limited, offer "found-object combinatorial for a natural keepsake instead, or shift to a travel hunt toward Andersonville for shop access?"
